A NASA-Informed Framework for Carbon Sequestration and Ecological Recovery

Problem Statement

The Destruction of Natural Carbon Sinks, The bombing and ground operations have devastated Gaza's primary natural ecosystems the coastal sand dunes (the "Green Belt") and the Wadi Gaza wetland. These areas were vital carbon sinks, actively absorbing and storing CO₂ from the atmosphere. Their destruction has not only halted this function but has also released stored carbon back into the air through burning and decomposition, turning these landscapes from a carbon solution into a carbon source. Soil Degradation and the Loss of Agricultural Carbon Scientific Explanation: The conflict has physically degraded and compacted Gaza's soils. This damage destroys the soil microbiome, which is crucial for storing carbon as soil organic matter. As this microbiome dies and soil structure collapses, the stored carbon is oxidized and released as CO₂. Furthermore, this degradation cripples the land's future ability to support crops or natural vegetation, preventing future carbon drawdown.

Potential Solutions & Innovations

Gaza's rubble is more than waste it's a resource waiting to be transformed. By recycling debris into usable materials, communities can rebuild homes, schools, and hospitals while creating jobs, reducing costs, and turning destruction into an opportunity for resilience.

Turning Waste into Resources

  • Large amounts of rubble left after building demolitions.
  • Limited access to fresh construction materials.
  • Recycling rubble saves resources, reduces waste, and creates jobs.

1. Data-Driven Re-vegetation

  • Strategic Planting of Resilient Species:
    Based on the mapping, we will launch a large-scale planting initiative. Using NASA SMAP (Soil Moisture Active Passive) data, we will identify locations with sufficient soil moisture to ensure the survival of new saplings. We will plant native, drought-resistant species with high carbon sequestration rates, such as deep-rooted dune grasses for the Green Belt and native reeds and sedges for the Wadi Gaza wetland.

2. Enhanced Weathering for Soil Repair and CO₂ Sequestration

  • This solution tackles both CO₂ removal and soil restoration simultaneously through a process called Enhanced Weathering, When finely crushed silicate rocks (like basalt) are spread on land, they naturally react with CO₂ and water in the atmosphere. This chemical reaction forms stable carbonate minerals, effectively locking away the carbon for thousands of years while releasing beneficial nutrients into the soil.

3. Soil Remediation and Carbon Sequestration

  • We will amend degraded agricultural and natural soils with crushed basalt rock dust. This directly removes CO₂ from the atmosphere through the chemical reaction described above.

4. Precision Application Guided by Satellite Data

  • We will use NASA Landsat and AVIRIS data to map areas of severe soil degradation and nutrient deficiency. This allows for the precise application of basalt dust only where it is needed most, maximizing efficiency and impact. The basalt dust also replenishes essential nutrients like potassium, calcium, and magnesium, restoring soil fertility for future farming Ecological Mapping Using Satellite Data.
