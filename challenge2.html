Gaza's Water Crisis and Scientific Solutions

Problem Statement

Over-extraction of groundwater and seawater intrusion. Gaza's coastal aquifer is being pumped at more than twice its natural recharge rate. As freshwater levels drop, salty water from the Mediterranean Sea intrudes into the aquifer. Climate change worsens this through sea level rise, increased heat, and prolonged droughts. Destroyed water infrastructure and lack of drinking water. Around 75% of Gaza's wells have been destroyed, while fuel shortages prevent operation of the remaining ones. This has created an acute thirst crisis. Rising demand under climate stress, with rapid population growth and rising temperatures, water demand is soaring while supply is shrinking. Extreme weather events (heatwaves and droughts) intensify the shortage.

Potential Solutions & Innovations

Gaza's water crisis demands urgent, science-driven solutions. From desalination and atmospheric water harvesting to precision irrigation and wastewater reuse, these approaches supported by NASA satellite data offer practical ways to restore safe water and build long-term resilience.

  • Implement artificial aquifer recharge using collected rainwater stored in reservoirs and infiltration basins.
  • Mobile solar-powered desalination units: Small, decentralized RO-based systems powered by solar energy. Can be distributed across neighborhoods, reducing dependence on a single large facility that can fail.
  • Solar-Powered Atmospheric Water Generators: Devices that extract drinking water directly from Gaza's high humidity. Provide a sustainable source of water while easing pressure on the aquifer.
  • Deploy precision irrigation systems guided by satellite data to minimize waste. Expand biological wastewater treatment to reuse treated sewage water for agriculture instead of relying on groundwater.

Statistics

In Gaza, water has become a matter of survival. With most wells destroyed and desalination plants shut down by fuel shortages, clean drinking water is almost out of reach.
